Modular Group \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\)

Vector Spaces \(M_k(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\) and \(S_k(SL_2(\mathbb{Z}))\)

Hecke Operators \(T_m\)
Let \(\mathbb{H} = \{z \in \mathbb{C} : Re(z) > 0\}\) be the complex upper half plane.
Let \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z}) = \left\{\begin{pmatrix}a & b \\ c & d\end{pmatrix}: a,b,c,d \in \mathbb{Z} \textup{ and } ad-bc = 1\right\}\) be the special linear group (modular group).
Modular forms are analytic functions defined on the complex upper half plane \(\mathbb{H}\), that satisfy the following two conditions:
  1. \(f\left(\frac{az+b}{cz+d}\right) = (cz+d)^kf(z)\) for some fixed \(k \in \mathbb{Z}\), for all \(z \in \mathbb{H}\), and for all \(\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d \\ \end{pmatrix} \in SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\).
  2. \(\exists K \in \mathbb{R}\) such that \(\lim_{y \rightarrow \infty}|f(x+iy)| < \infty\). That is, \(f\) is bounded as the argument approaches \(i\infty\).
The value k in the first condition is called the weight of the modular form.

The unique properties of \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\) allow condition (1) to be reduced to two more easily verified conditions:
  • \(f(z+1) = f(z) \ \forall z \in \mathbb{H}\)
  • \(f(\frac{-1}{z}) = z^kf(z) \ \forall z \in \mathbb{H}\) and for some fixed constant \(k \in \mathbb{Z}\)
We can also define modular forms for special subgroups of \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\) called congruence subgroups, which do not fully satisfy condition 1, but satisfy it for the congruence subgroup.

If a modular form \(f\) satisfies condition 2 such that \(\lim_{y \rightarrow \infty}|f(x+iy)|= 0\), we call it a cusp form.

It follows from the definition of a modular form that if \(f\) and \(g\) are modular forms of weight k, and \(c \in \mathbb{C}\) is any scalar, then \(cf+g\) is also a modular form of weight k. Thus, modular forms of weight k form a vector space. We denote the vector space of weight k modular forms as \(M_k(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\) and the space of weight k cusp forms as \(S_k(SL_2(\mathbb{Z}))\).

Why Study Modular Forms?

Modular forms have been studied by mathematicians for over 100 years!

Modular forms provide an essential connection between Number Theory, Abstract Algebra, and Geometry. It is the starting place in an active area of research called the Langlands program.

Modular forms are ubiquitous in modern mathematics, and they have a vast number of interesting properties! One is compelled to study their beauty irrespective of any subdiscipline.

The only prerequisites for studying modular forms are elementary number theory, linear and abstract algebra. Complex analysis is recommended but one can probably get away without taking that course.
