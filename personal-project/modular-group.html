The modular group \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\)

Finitely Generated

\(SL_2(Z)\) is generated by the following matrices \(S\) and \(T\).
\(S = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & -1 \\ 1 & 0 \end{pmatrix}\) and \(T = \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 1 \\ 0 & 1 \end{pmatrix}\).
where \(S\) has order 4 and \(T\) has infinite order.
This makes it easy to prove facts about all the matrices of \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\). One needs only to prove it for \(S\) and \(T\) and then show that the property being true for two matrices implies it is true for products of those matrices.

Subgroups of the modular group

We can construct special subgroups of \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\) called congruence subgroups. If we let \[\Gamma(N) = \left\{\begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d\end{pmatrix}\in SL_2(\mathbb{Z}): \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d\end{pmatrix} \equiv \begin{pmatrix} 1 & 0 \\ 0 & 1\end{pmatrix} (\textup{ mod } N)\right\}\] Then a group \(\Gamma\) is a congruence subgroup if and only if \(\Gamma(N) \subseteq \Gamma\) for some \(N \in \mathbb{Z}_+\).

The Modular group acting on the upper half plane

We look at \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\) as a group acting on the topological space of the upper half-plane \(\mathbb{H}\) using the group action
\(\gamma z \mapsto \frac{az+b}{cz+d}\) with \(\gamma = \begin{pmatrix} a & b \\ c & d\end{pmatrix} \in SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\)
Let \(\mathcal{F} = \left\{z \in \mathbb{H}: -\frac{1}{2} < Re(z) < \frac{1}{2},\ |z| \geq 1\right\}\).
Then \(\mathcal{F}\) is a fundamental domain for the action of \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\) on \(\mathbb{H}\). That is the region satisfies
  • Every complex number \(\tau \in \mathbb{H}\) is \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\) equivalent to some \(z \in \mathcal {F}\)
  • Two points \(z_1, z_2 \in \mathcal{F}\) are \(SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\) equivalent if and only if they are on the boundary of \(\mathcal{F}\).
  • The boundary of \(\mathcal{F}\) is a finite union of smooth curves.


To make a fundamental domain for congruence subgroups \(\Gamma \subset SL_2(\mathbb{Z})\), we take a minimal set of coset representatives, and create a larger fundamental domain by multiplying \(\mathcal{F}\) with elements from these cosets and attaching the areas together into one connected piece.
